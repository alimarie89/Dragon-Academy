The five physics

I Truth Will Not Arrive Gently It arrives as sensation, not clarity ▼

Truth rarely arrives as relief. It arrives as a tightening chest, a late no, a pull toward contact you want to deny, an impulse to manage the moment or disappear from it. Most of us were trained to override these signals — to call that override maturity. Here, destabilization is treated as signal. The work is to stay present long enough for sensation to become intelligible before deciding what to do with it.

II You Are Not Meant to Handle Truth Alone Relationship is the primary stabilizer ▼

When something destabilizing arises, most people isolate or perform coherence so no one sees the mess. Dragon Academy assumes humans integrate truth better in relationship — not by being rescued or analyzed, but by staying in contact while something real is happening. Truth becomes dangerous not because it's too much, but because it's carried alone.

III Power Will Show You Yourself Power is a mirror, not an identity ▼

Power reveals patterns faster than introspection ever could. When power is present you learn quickly where you override yourself, submit too early, dominate to feel safe, or lose contact with your own truth. The work isn't to eliminate power or perform being "good" with it. The work is to stay conscious while power moves — so it becomes information instead of compulsion.

IV Your Body Has a Different Clock Than Your Mind Pacing is containment, not avoidance ▼

Your body knows before you can explain. Your nervous system reacts before you have language. Clarity lags behind sensation; meaning arrives last, if you allow it. Pauses are not avoidance. Slowing down is not weakness. Delayed clarity is not incompetence. Time and pacing are how the system integrates truth without overwhelming itself.

V You Can Lead Yourself Without Overriding Yourself Orientation before action ▼

Most people confuse leadership with control — deciding before feeling, acting before listening, overriding internal signals to appear decisive. Dragon Academy trains a different capacity: orientation. The ability to locate yourself accurately in what's happening before you decide what to do. To feel first. To act from contact rather than command. Leadership, before it's about others, is about how you relate to yourself under pressure.

Core concepts — tap to explore

01 Instinct Authority returned to the body ▼

Sensation, hunger, aggression, eros, and protection arriving as pre-verbal intelligence — before thought has a chance to manage it.

Instinct is what the body already knows before the mind has organized it into a story. In Dragon Academy, instinct isn't metaphor — it's literal body intelligence. The tightening throat before you say yes to something you mean no. The pull toward someone before you've named why. The recoil before you've identified the threat.

Most of us learned to distrust or override instinct — to call our body's signals "irrational," to smooth sensation into something socially acceptable before speaking. The work here is to catch the signal before the override, and stay with it long enough that it becomes navigable.

Pre-verbal Sensation Hunger Aggression Protection Eros

Ali's definition: instinct is authority returned to the body. When instinct is alive, five things come online as pre-verbal intelligence: sensation (direct body knowing), directionality (toward or away, without story), hunger (desire as life force), aggression (protective force, not hostility), and eros (the pull toward contact and aliveness).

What blocks instinct isn't ignorance — it's the accumulated scar tissue of having been punished, shamed, or ignored when your instinct told the truth. The box of self (concept 02) is what happens when instinct learns to hide.

Why it matters: Every sophisticated skill in this course — truth-telling, consent, desire, repair — runs on instinct as the underlying operating system. If that signal is cloudy, everything downstream is navigating by story instead of sensation.

Practices from this session

Instinct Scan Quiet pause: tracking what the body already knows, before language. What's the sensation? Where does it live? Is there a direction in it?
The First No / First Yes Noticing the body's first response to a question before the mind can edit it — and reporting that, rather than the edited version.
02 Box of Self How instinct learned to survive ▼

The habituated survival strategies your instincts learned to use when your early environment rewarded certain shapes of being and punished others.

Over time, instincts iterate. You act a certain way, get a response, and that response teaches your system what's safe to show and what must be hidden. These patterns become your "box of self" — the habituated behaviors that developed not from choice, but from the accumulated intelligence of how to belong.

The box isn't a flaw. It worked. It was probably necessary. The question is whether it still serves you, or whether it's now running the show at moments when you could choose differently.

Your box of self is the shape your instinct learned to take. If being vulnerable got you love, you learned to lead with vulnerability. If making others responsible for your feelings got you attention, that became a pattern. If going quiet kept things safe, silence became a strategy. None of this is moral failure — it's nervous system intelligence.

What Dragon Academy is asking: Can you see the box? Can you step outside it — even briefly — so it becomes a tool instead of a container you're trapped in?

Light vs. shadow in the box: most boxes have a "light" presentation (the version you lead with) and a "shadow" (the version that runs when heat is up). The shadow isn't bad — it's just less chosen.

Practices from this session

Box Mapping Naming your patterns: What do you do when heat rises? What do you do to belong? What do you hide? What do you lead with?
Light / Shadow Dyad Partners name their box's light expression and its shadow twin — the version that runs without consent.
03 Truth-Telling Saying what's happening before you've made it acceptable ▼

Speaking from internal state without packaging it for palatability — the discipline of naming what's actually happening before you've decided what to do about it.

Truth-telling in Dragon Academy isn't about radical honesty as a performance or weaponizing your inner state at others. It's the discipline of catching what's true before the social mind has a chance to edit it into something safer.

The moment your chest tightens, the moment you feel the pull or the recoil — that's the truth. What you do next (translate it into acceptable language, explain it away, perform composure) is the box of self operating. Truth-telling trains the gap between signal and performance.

"Spicy relational truth" — the phrase used in class — is the category of truths that have social cost. The attraction you didn't mention. The no that came late. The irritation you called "fine." The truth that would change the room if you said it out loud.

Dragon Academy doesn't require anyone to say everything. It trains the capacity to know what's true without immediately suppressing or exploding it — so that when speaking becomes available, it comes from contact rather than reactivity.

The key distinction: truth-telling isn't about speaking everything. It's about not lying to yourself about what's happening while it's happening.

Practices from this session

Spicy Truth Circle Each person names one truth they've been holding that has social cost. No explanation, no softening. Report the signal.
State Before Story Dyad practice: describe what's happening in your body first, before the story about what it means.
04 Interplay Real-time attention practice under relational pressure ▼

A live contact exercise that shows you exactly where someone else can hijack your center — and trains you to stay in contact without losing yourself.

Interplay is the primary training tool of the course. Two people face each other and practice staying in contact while applying graduated pressure — physical proximity, intensity, presence, uncertainty. The goal isn't to be unmoved. The goal is to feel the impact and stay present anyway.

What usually happens: you either merge (lose your center in theirs), separate (protect by pulling back into your head), or freeze (become still in a way that's actually distance). Interplay trains a fourth option: staying in genuine contact while remaining in yourself.

There are multiple forms of Interplay used across the course: standard Interplay (face to face, graduated intensity), Authorship Interplay (practicing staying the author of your choices while someone tries to destabilize that), and Reverse Interplay (playing the person who holds power, who creates the field).

The key concept underneath all versions: shared spine. Both people can be in contact AND have their own center. This isn't a compromise — it's a skill. The spine is not rigidity. It's the thread of self that stays continuous even when someone else's nervous system is pressing against yours.

Variations used in class

Standard Interplay Pairs face each other and increase contact slowly — eye contact, proximity, presence — while tracking what their nervous system does.
Shared Spine / Building Shared Spine Partners practice staying in their own center while allowing genuine connection — neither merging nor protecting.
Reverse Interplay One person holds the organizing presence; the other practices receiving it. Explores the relationship between attention-out and attention-in.
05 Authorship / Authority You are already writing your life ▼

Author, authority, and authenticity share the same root — to originate. You are already writing your life. The only question is whether you're doing it consciously or by default.

The etymology: author, authority, and authenticity all descend from the Latin augere — to originate, to cause to grow. Authentic authority isn't granted from outside; it's what happens when you stop outsourcing your choices to external systems and become the originating source of your own actions.

Most people operate as characters in someone else's story — shaped by what their family expected, what their culture approved, what their box of self learned to perform. Authorship is the practice of consciously taking up the pen.

Authorship doesn't mean ignoring others or relationships. It means being the one who chooses your response, rather than the one who reacts. This is a felt state, not a philosophical position. You know when you're in authorship — there's a particular quality of presence, of agency, of groundedness in your own body that's absent when you're in reaction.

The morality piece connects here directly: when moral authority is external ("this is objectively wrong"), it's borrowed, and borrowed authority collapses under heat. When it's internal ("this violates what I stand for"), it creates gravity. Clean authorship means owning your values as yours — not as cosmic law.

Practices from this session

Authorship Interplay One person practices being the author of their presence while their partner attempts to destabilize that — through intensity, challenge, invitation, or pressure. Track where you lose the pen.
Morality Origin Check Name a value you hold. Ask: "Is this mine or borrowed?" Feel the difference in the body between "I choose this" vs. "I'm supposed to."
06 Settling Styles How the nervous system returns after activation ▼

People need different things to regulate after intensity — and forcing someone to settle your way is itself a form of covert control.

After activation — an argument, a charged moment, an emotional peak — the nervous system reaches for something to settle. But different people need different things: some need relational contact, some need physical movement, some need to accomplish a task, some need to be alone with their own inner process, some need cognitive reframing.

When you assume your settling style is the right one and apply pressure for someone to settle your way, you're not being caring — you're trying to regulate your own discomfort by regulating them. This is one of the quieter, harder-to-see forms of coercion in close relationships.

The five settling styles identified in the course:

Relational — needs connection with another person to settle. Words, eye contact, presence. The danger: using connection to bypass processing.
Somatic — needs to move the activation through the body. Walk, shake, exercise, breathe. The danger: physical release without integration.
Functional — needs to do something useful. Task, productivity, accomplishment. The danger: escape into function rather than presence.
Intrinsic — needs time alone with their own inner process. Space, silence, internal integration. The danger: isolation that becomes disconnection.
Cognitive — needs to understand what happened before settling is possible. Reframe, contextualize, make meaning. The danger: intellectualization that avoids feeling.

No style is superior. The work is knowing yours, communicating it, and giving others space for theirs — especially when yours and theirs are in conflict.

Connection to morality: When someone says "you should come talk to me right now" after a fight and their partner needs space — that "should" is usually disguising an unmet settling need, not a genuine ethical concern.

07 Threat Real risk vs. nervous system alarm ▼

The nervous system's alarm evolved for survival, not precision — discernment means distinguishing real danger from inherited pattern, and responding rather than reacting.

When belonging or connection feel threatened — when the nervous system perceives danger to identity, relationship, status, or physical safety — the body activates. Old survival strategies take over: fight, flight, freeze, fawn, or fix.

The problem: the nervous system can't reliably distinguish between actual threat and pattern-match to old wounds. The person who raises their voice might remind your body of an abusive parent. Silence from a partner might feel like abandonment from childhood. The system fires the same alarm.

Three questions that help at the moment of threat activation:

1. What is my nervous system actually afraid of? Name it specifically — not "I'm overwhelmed" but "I'm afraid you're going to leave" or "I'm afraid I'm wrong about this and that makes me dangerous."

2. Is this a real threat or a pattern? Is there actual evidence right now, or is this old data?

3. What does responsive rather than reactive look like here? You can feel the activation fully AND choose your response. The goal isn't calm. The goal is choice inside the heat.

A useful reframe: pressure (external power pressing against you) doesn't have to register as threat. Pressure can be information. The same activation that causes collapse can become fuel for presence, if the nervous system has been trained to metabolize it.

08 Vulnerability Being locatable — a form of relational power ▼

Not the performance of openness, but the calibrated act of letting yourself be found — the amount and timing of which determines whether it builds intimacy or creates chaos.

Vulnerability in this course isn't emotional exposure for its own sake. It's the precision of being locatable — letting others feel you, find you, make contact with what's actually happening for you. When you're opaque, people can't locate you. When they can't locate you, real connection is impossible regardless of how much time you spend together.

This is a form of relational power. The person who can be found, who can let others feel them, who sends clean signals — that person creates the conditions for genuine contact. Opacity isn't self-protection. It's isolation with company.

There are two failure modes, not one:

Under-vulnerability (opacity): The person who can't be located. Flat affect, withheld state, perpetual composure. Others can't find them. Connection stays thin. This looks like strength; it's actually a form of distance.

Over-vulnerability (discharge): Emotional output that floods the field, doesn't serve the relationship, and often functions as nervous system regulation at others' expense. The overshare that leaves the room confused. The constant disclosure that builds character trust but destroys competency trust. This looks like openness; it's often avoidance in disguise.

The sweet spot: vulnerability that is attuned — calibrated to the moment, the relationship, and what actually serves connection rather than what relieves internal pressure.

The character trust / competency trust distinction: Vulnerability builds character trust. But if it reveals you don't have your act together, competency trust drops. Both matter. The people you most want to work with, be with, trust — they have both. Neither performs invulnerability nor performs openness. They're just real, at the right depth, at the right time.

09 Power Range of options × capacity to act on them ▼

The range of choices available to you, plus the actual capacity to act on them — and none of it matters if you're reactive, because reactive means small.

Power has two dimensions that often get confused. The horizontal: range — how many options you can actually see, how many different paths exist for you in this moment. And the vertical: capacity — can you actually execute? Do you have the skill, the resources, the embodied ability to move in those directions?

The power Dragon Academy cares about most is relational power — not dominance or control, but the ability to let others feel you and let yourself be affected without losing your center. Power and authentic intimacy aren't opposed. They come from the same source.

Working definition: Power dynamics are the invisible choreography of influence unfolding within a shared relational field. Whenever two people interact, attention moves, nervous systems respond, each person adjusts or resists adjusting. This is always happening — naming it just makes it navigable.

Key insight: You're small when reactive. You can be loud and reactive (domination) or quiet and reactive (collapse) — neither is powerful. Power is the capacity to feel the full activation and still choose. That's what "clean danger" means: instinct is felt but not blindly enacted, truth is spoken without performance, power is expressed as range rather than domination.

Personal power, relational power, and reality power (what you can actually do in the world) are related but distinct. What connects them: the nervous system's ability to hold voltage without being taken over by it.

Working with power in the dojo

Power Mapping In any given interaction: who has power? How? What kind? Notice what your body does when power is uneven. Notice where you inflate or collapse.
The Responsive vs. Reactive Check When activated: "Is this responsive or reactive?" Responsive = feels it, stays present, chooses. Reactive = feels it, loses options.
10 Morality & Integrity Owned values vs. borrowed rules ▼

Morality outsourced ("this is objectively wrong") collapses under heat. Morality owned ("this violates what I stand for") creates gravity. Reclaiming the second is reclaiming authorship.

Dragon Academy doesn't dismiss ethics or tell people values don't matter. It asks a more precise question: are your values actually yours? When you say "you shouldn't do that" — is that coming from genuine integrity, or from a rule you inherited that you've never examined? There's a felt difference in the body between "I choose this" and "I'm supposed to."

The deeper observation: moralization is often a nervous system strategy. "Should/shouldn't" language frequently disguises an unmet settling need or an attempt to make someone else responsible for your activation. Naming it as such isn't cynical — it's precise.

From the class discussion: "When a person doesn't know that their morality is their own — when it's I'm choosing this — when you're in a place of 'this is not objectively true, all meaning is made up,' when you come from that stance, you communicate in a way of: this is my experience, these are my values, and I'm in the power of I'm holding those values. I'm not non-consensually enforcing my story on you."

The relational effect: people who operate from owned values are actually safer to be around. They can disagree without trying to convert you. They can hold ground without needing to be right. They can be wrong and update without shame.

The irony to watch: you can be righteous about people being righteous. Intolerant of intolerance. Moralistic about moralism. The work is also on yourself. That's the Landmark framing: "Nothing we're saying is true. But try it on."

11 Repair & Feedback Rupture is not failure. It's the work. ▼

True feedback is observational data in service of a shared commitment — not emotional discharge, not a demand for someone to change, not a covert request for your nervous system to be managed.

Rupture in relationship is inevitable. The question isn't whether it will happen — it's whether you have the skill to repair it. Dragon Academy treats rupture as part of the curriculum, not evidence that something went wrong.

Repair means: owning your contribution first, making space for the other's experience before rushing to resolution, and staying in contact even when it's uncomfortable. Adults initiate repair rather than waiting to be pursued.

The feedback framework: Clean feedback has three requirements.

1. An observable behavior — something you could put on video ("When you interrupted me three times in the last hour...")

2. A shared commitment it violates — not "because it's wrong" but "because we agreed that..." or "because what I care about here is..."

3. Radical ownership of your own state — you're not reporting an objective truth about them, you're reporting your experience and making a request. The moment feedback becomes "you made me feel" it's slipped into projection.

What passes as feedback in most relationships is actually a mix of projection (your old material landing on them), preference (you wanting things done your way), judgment (a moral conclusion about who they are), and emotional discharge (releasing your activation at them).

Repair sequence: (1) Acknowledge the impact, (2) own your contribution, (3) invite their experience, (4) move toward resolution only after both people feel seen.

The arc isn't a syllabus. It's an unfolding.

"Power is not about being loud, dominating, or in control. It is about range — how many choices you can actually see — and capacity — whether you can act on them. Both collapse when the nervous system is reactive."

Power shows you yourself faster than almost anything else. Dragon Academy doesn't try to eliminate power dynamics or pretend they don't exist. It treats them as diagnostic — information about how the system is actually functioning.

Lenses on power

I Power as Range × Capacity The Dragon Academy working definition ▼

Range: How many options can you actually see? How many paths exist for you in this moment? A person with narrow range sees one response to a situation — and enacts it automatically. A person with wide range sees many options, including doing nothing.

Capacity: Can you actually execute? Do you have the skill, the embodied ability, the inner resources to move in those directions? Range without capacity is fantasy. Capacity without range is compulsion.

The nervous system modifier: Both collapse when you're reactive. Reactive doesn't mean loud — it means your options have narrowed to one. You can be loud and reactive (domination mode) or quiet and reactive (collapse mode). Neither is power. Power is the capacity to feel the full activation and still choose.

"Power dynamics are the invisible choreography of influence unfolding within a shared relational field." Whenever two people interact, attention moves, nervous systems respond, each person adjusts or resists adjusting. This is always happening. Naming it makes it navigable.

II Three Kinds of Power Personal, relational, and power in the world ▼

Personal power is internal — your capacity to self-regulate, self-author, feel what you feel without being driven by it, and act from your own values rather than borrowed rules or inherited fear. This is power over the story you tell about yourself. It develops through practices like instinct training, Box of Self work, and authorship.

Relational power is what Dragon Academy cares most about: the ability to let others feel you and let yourself be affected by others without losing your center. It is not dominance. It is contact. The person with relational power is locatable — their signal is clean, their presence is felt. They can hold the field without needing to control it.

Power in the world (sometimes called structural or social power) refers to resources, access, position, and influence that exist before the conversation starts. Race, gender, money, title, credentials — these shift the field before anyone opens their mouth. Dragon Academy doesn't pretend structural power doesn't exist; it asks how you show up within and across power differentials without either hiding from them or hiding behind them.

Reflection practice

Power Inventory In a relationship or situation you're navigating: What type of power do you have? What type does the other person have? Are they the same type? Are you using your actual power, or avoiding it? Are you inflating, or collapsing?
III Power Up / Power Down Structural position vs. embodied power ▼

Power up refers to someone in a structurally elevated position: the boss, the teacher, the facilitator, the parent, the person with the resource the other needs.

Power down refers to the structurally subordinate position: the employee, the student, the participant, the child, the one seeking the resource.

These are real. And they can be navigated consciously from either position. A conscious yes when you're in the power-down position is still a full expression of personal power. A conscious no from the power-up position can be an act of care, not dominance. Position is structural. Power is embodied.

The key risk: People in power-up positions often don't feel powerful — they feel responsible, exposed, or scrutinized. People in power-down positions often don't feel powerless — they feel sharp, agile, invisible-powerful. Neither structural position determines how much embodied agency is available to you in the moment. It just shapes the terrain.

DA working principle: Don't collapse structural position and personal power. You can be power-up and collapsed. You can be power-down and fully alive. The work is staying conscious to both realities simultaneously.

IV Dangerous vs. Powerful A distinction worth sitting with ▼

One conversation that lives in Dragon Academy: "Dangerous swipes one way. Powerful swipes both ways."

Powerful means having range. Someone who can be soft or fierce, vulnerable or fierce, yielding or fierce — and chooses consciously. Power swipes in all directions. It's not locked into one mode.

Dangerous names something slightly different: the quality of being hard to manipulate, difficult to coerce, not easily moved by flattery or threat. Something that can't be captured or controlled. This is not the same as volatility or domination — it's more like a tree that bends in the storm without breaking. Roots deep enough that the wind is interesting rather than terrifying.

Some people in Dragon Academy find "dangerous" more alive than "powerful" — it points at something specific: freedom from being managed by others' agendas. Others find it misleading because danger implies harm. The question isn't which word is right. The question is: what quality are you actually training for?

V Power in Interplay The Interplay definition — attention as power ▼

In Interplay, power has two components:

1. The ability to put your attention somewhere and hold it there in the face of challenge. This sounds simple and isn't. Notice how quickly attention gets pulled away, hijacked, collapsed, or flooded in real interaction. The capacity to choose where attention goes — and keep it there under pressure — is a trainable form of power.

2. The ability to influence where someone else's attention is, or the quality of that attention. Not manipulation — but presence. When you're fully in contact with someone, their attention naturally orients toward you. When you're scattered, withheld, or performing, attention slides off you.

The Interplay freeze — "the hole" — is power loss in real time. When the hole opens, you disappear from the field even while still physically present. The recovery practice ("you have a nose") is a literal act of reclaiming power: put attention outward, on something real, right now.

Practice

Attention as Power Drill In Interplay: practice the deliberate choice to put your attention on your partner and hold it there as pressure increases. Notice what pulls you away — judgment, self-monitoring, desire, discomfort, the urge to be understood. Each pull-away is the moment power redistributes. Reclaiming it each time is the drill.
VI Power & Surrender Acceptance as a form of power, not its absence ▼

A thread that has surfaced but hasn't been fully followed: fighting what is — the weather, the wall, the feeling you're having, the situation you're in — is often a form of powerlessness. The surfer who fights the wave gets tumbled. The one who rides it, or who knows when to let it take them, survives it.

Acceptance is not passive. It's not agreement. It's the specific capacity to stop spending energy resisting what cannot be changed in this moment — so that energy is available for what can. Research on psychological flexibility (ACT, acceptance and commitment therapy) finds this to be one of the strongest predictors of wellbeing and effective action: the ability to hold difficult experiences without fighting them OR being controlled by them.

The DA frame: Surrender is not giving up your power. It's releasing the grip on the story that power requires you to control the outcome. You can be fully alive, fully in contact with what's happening, and not insisting that it be different — and act from that place. That combination is rare and strong.

A concrete distinction: yielding (conscious choice to allow) vs. collapsing (nervous system overwhelm). They can look the same from outside. They feel entirely different from inside. Yielding expands range. Collapsing narrows it.

VII Power & Intimacy Are the Same River Why the most powerful thing you can do is let yourself be felt ▼

The connection that hasn't been said plainly yet in the group: power and authentic intimacy are not opposites. They come from the same source.

Hot sex, deep emotional contact, spiritual intimacy, creative partnership — all of these run on the same underlying capacity: staying conscious under heat. The ability to feel more without losing yourself. The ability to let someone in without disappearing. The ability to be moved without being swept away.

People who avoid power tend to also avoid deep intimacy — the same self-protective strategies that keep them from being seen as powerful also keep them from being genuinely close. People who use power coercively often can't be intimate at all — real intimacy requires being actually affected, which control-based power refuses.

The capacity Dragon Academy is building — staying conscious while eros, intensity, desire, or conflict are alive — is the same capacity that makes love, sex, friendship, and leadership all richer. They're not different skills. They're different applications of the same trained nervous system.

Reflection

The Intimacy-Power Mirror Where in your life are you protecting yourself from power? Where are you protecting yourself from intimacy? Notice whether those places overlap. They usually do. The question isn't: how do I become more powerful OR more intimate? It's: what in my nervous system still believes that being fully present is dangerous?
VIII What Research Adds Relevant frameworks from outside the dojo ▼

French & Raven's power bases (1959): Identified six sources of power — reward, coercive, legitimate (position), expert, referent (relationship/charisma), and informational. Useful for seeing that power has multiple currencies. In DA terms: most of us over-rely on one or two and are blind to the others we actually have.

Amy Cuddy on embodied power: Posture, breath, and body state influence hormonal patterns that affect confidence and actual performance. Power isn't only something you have — it's something you inhabit or evacuate through your body. This aligns with the DA nervous system frame: power isn't a fixed trait, it's a somatic state.

Dacher Keltner on the power paradox: People gain influence through warmth, care, and attunement — then often lose it as they acquire power because those qualities erode. The "power paradox" is that the qualities that earn you influence are the ones power tends to strip away. DA's emphasis on staying affected, staying relational, staying locatable is a direct antidote to this erosion.

Attachment theory and power: People with anxious attachment often use indirect influence (compliance, withdrawal, emotional escalation) because direct power-expression was unsafe early on. People with avoidant attachment often over-rely on one-directional power — self-sufficiency, dominance — because needing others felt dangerous. Dragon Academy's relational power training is attachment healing by another name: learning that you can be powerful AND need people, strong AND soft, influential AND affected.

Polyvagal theory (Porges): The ventral vagal state — social engagement, safety, connection — is itself a form of power. When you're genuinely regulated and present, you create conditions in others. The regulated nervous system is literally influential. This is why a calm, grounded facilitator changes a room's temperature. Safety is a form of power few people think of as power.
